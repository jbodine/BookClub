Behavioral economists call this phenomenon "asymmetric loss aversion?" Call it what you will, the undeniable fact is that we react disproportionately to falling markets as compared with rising ones. [70]

Chapter 5

Your appropriate behavior with respect to equity investments—and not the relative "performance" of those investments—is the variable that will govern your financial success. [114]

Most people put huge amounts of energy into variables they can't control, and which won't have much if any real effect on their total lifetime return. [114]

Your retirement savings plan has to be treated like your most important monthly bill. Because, of course, that's exactly what it is. [120]

From 1966 to 1982, with dividends reinvested, the S&P 500 only returned something like 5% a year (this was the Viet-nam/Watergate/OPEC/stagflation era, and it featured in 1973-74 the biggest bear market of the postwar period up to that time). A generation later, in 2000-2012, the net return of equities for 13 years was nonexistent other than from dividends. Both these dark periods came after spectacular, multi-year bull markets. The point is that the average 10%- 12% returns (of large- and small-company equities, respectively) are, after all, just that: averages, which means that reality spends about as much time below them as above them. And neither you nor anyone else can time that reality. [121-122]

Observe that in switching funds, most people are trying to close the performance gap by manipulating a variable they can't control. Typically, they get out of funds that have underperformed in recent years (and may therefore be cyclically about to do very well) and into funds that have shot the lights out in recent years (and may therefore be about to go cold, particularly if they get huge inflows of performance-chasing money that they can't invest well). This may sound like a vicious cycle, but it's actually a downward spiral. [122]

Concentrating on behavioral responses you can control: increasing your monthly investment, planning to work a year or two longer (even if it's only part-time) and/or trimming your going-in withdrawal rate. [122]

Bear markets (big sales) can't come along too often, or go down too much. [130]

You may also be able simply to economize for a while, reducing your annual withdrawal from the "nice to have" down to the "need to have" level. You might even consider taking on some part-time work until the rains come again. I'm not suggesting that these fallback positions are especially pleasant to contemplate; I'm also not saying that they'll ever be necessary. The point is simply that if it starts to feel like your plane is tail-spinning, you don't have to hold the throttle wide open until you hit the ground. You've got options. [134]

The right time to buy equities is always when you have the money. The right time to sell them is when (and if) you need the money. Otherwise... just let 'em grow. [135]

"Investment" has to do with the identification of value. Speculation is almost invariably a bet on the continuation of a price trend. Price and value are always inversely correlated. [170]
