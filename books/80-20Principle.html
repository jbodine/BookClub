The 80/20 Principle

Written by Richard Koch

Chapter 1

We tend to expect that all causes will have roughly the same significance. That all customers are equally valuable. [p.10]

We tend to assume that 50 percent of causes or inputs will account for 50 percent of results or outputs. There seems to be a natural expectation that causes and results are generally equally balanced, but this “50/50 fallacy” is one of our most inaccurate and yet deeply rooted mental maps. [p.10]

Most of the time, we do not realize the extent to which some resources, but only a small minority, are super-productive — the vital few vs. the trivial many. [p.12]

The universe is not a straight line [p.14]

The 80/20 principle, like chaos theory, is based around the idea of non linearity. [p.14]

Small initial influences can become greatly multiplied and produce highly unexpected results.[p.15]

A small lead early on can turn into a larger lead or a dominate position later on, until equilibrium is distributed and another small force then exerts a disproportionate influence.[p.16]

(One of the messages of chaos theory is that equality ends in dominance.) A 51/49 split is inherently unstable and tends to gravitate towards a 95/5, 99/1, or even 100/0 split. [p.17]

At any point, a majority of any phenomenon will be explained or caused by a minority of the actors participating in the phenomenon) [p.17]

The reasonable man adapts himself to the world. The unreasonable one persists in trying to adapt the world to himself. Therefore all progress depends on the unreasonable man - George Bernard Shaw [p.19]

The few things that work fantastically well should be identified, cultivated, nurtured, and multiplied. [p.20]
